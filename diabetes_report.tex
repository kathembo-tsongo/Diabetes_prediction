\section{Problem Definition}

Non-communicable diseases represent one of the most pressing public
health challenges of the 21st century, with diabetes mellitus among
the most prevalent and rapidly growing. Diabetes mellitus is a chronic
metabolic disorder that arises when the pancreas does not produce
enough insulin, or when the body cannot effectively use the insulin it
produces \citep{NIDDK2023}. Left undetected and untreated, chronically
elevated blood glucose levels progressively damage organs and body
systems, leading to severe complications including kidney failure,
blindness, cardiovascular disease, stroke, and lower limb amputation.

Despite the availability of effective diagnostic tools, a significant
proportion of people living with diabetes remain undiagnosed ---
often presenting to health facilities only at advanced stages of
the disease when complications have already set in. This diagnostic
gap represents both a clinical failure and a public health crisis,
particularly in low- and middle-income countries where healthcare
infrastructure and specialist capacity are limited.

This study asks: \textbf{Can we build a machine learning model that
accurately identifies whether a female patient aged 21 and above is at
risk of diabetes, using only routine clinical measurements available
at primary healthcare level?} The goal is to develop a binary
classification model --- one that predicts either diabetic (1) or
non-diabetic (0) --- that flags high-risk individuals early, enabling
timely medical intervention before complications arise. Such a model
could serve as a cost-effective, scalable screening tool in
resource-constrained healthcare settings.

\section{Business and Policy Relevance}

The relevance of this study extends well beyond academic interest ---
it addresses a significant and growing public health and economic
burden. Diabetes is one of the fastest growing global health
emergencies of the 21st century. The International Diabetes Federation
estimates that in 2021, \textbf{537 million adults aged 20--79 years}
were living with diabetes worldwide, a figure projected to rise to
783 million by 2045 \citep{IDF2021}. Direct health expenditures
due to diabetes are already approaching one trillion US dollars
annually.

The burden is particularly severe in Sub-Saharan Africa, including
Kenya. The World Health Organization reports that \textbf{54\% of
people living with diabetes in the African Region are undiagnosed}
\citep{WHO2022Africa}, representing the highest undiagnosed proportion
of any region globally. This means that more than half of all affected
individuals are unaware of their condition and are therefore receiving
no treatment --- placing them at high risk of preventable complications
and premature death.

From a policy perspective, a validated and deployable diabetes
prediction model would deliver the following benefits:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cost reduction:} Early detection and intervention
          significantly reduces the cost of managing late-stage
          diabetes complications such as dialysis, amputations,
          and cardiovascular procedures.
    \item \textbf{Capacity building:} Community health workers and
          nurses --- without specialist training --- could use the
          model as a decision-support tool during routine checkups,
          extending diagnostic reach without additional infrastructure.
    \item \textbf{Universal Health Coverage (UHC):} Kenya's UHC
          agenda prioritizes equitable access to preventive and
          primary care. A low-cost screening tool directly supports
          this objective by making early diagnosis accessible to
          underserved populations.
    \item \textbf{Data-driven policy:} Model outputs and patient
          risk profiles can inform targeted public health campaigns
          and resource allocation at county and national level.
\end{itemize}

\section{Context}

This problem is situated within a \textbf{clinical decision-support
context} --- specifically targeting primary healthcare facilities in
Kenya and similar resource-constrained environments, where specialist
endocrinologists are scarce, laboratory infrastructure is limited, and
patients often present late.

The dataset used in this study is the \textbf{PIMA Indians Diabetes
Dataset}, originally created by the National Institute of Diabetes
and Digestive and Kidney Diseases (NIDDK) and first used
by \citet{Smith1988} to test an early neural network model --- the
ADAP learning algorithm --- for forecasting diabetes onset in a
high-risk population of Pima Indian women. The dataset contains
diagnostic measurements from \textbf{768 female patients aged 21
and above}, recorded across nine clinical variables: number of
pregnancies, plasma glucose concentration, diastolic blood pressure,
triceps skinfold thickness, serum insulin level, body mass index
(BMI), diabetes pedigree function, age, and a binary outcome variable
(diabetic or not).

Although the dataset originates from a specific ethnic population
in the United States, its clinical variables --- glucose, BMI, blood
pressure, and insulin --- are universal biomarkers routinely measured
in primary care settings globally, including Kenya. The model developed
in this study is therefore generalizable in its clinical variables,
even if future work would benefit from validation on local Kenyan
population data.

Importantly, the model developed here is intended to
\textit{assist} --- not replace --- clinical judgment. It provides a
\textit{probability score} that signals elevated risk, prompting the
clinician to refer the patient for confirmatory blood glucose testing.
This human-in-the-loop design ensures that the model supports
responsible, ethical, and effective clinical practice.
